A02: Kinematics

Introduction

The purpose of this assignment is to demonstrate your knowledge of kinematics through exemplar exercises, specifically the following content

  • Vectors and vector operations
  • The use of vectors in describing constraints
  • The creation of vector-based expressions to describe key aspects of robotic systems such as the Jacobian and to demonstrate its use
  • Key uses of the quaternion in hinged and foldable systems

Instructions

Part 1

\[\begin{align} \vec{v}_1=&c_1 \hat{a}_x+c_2 \hat{a}_y+c_3 \hat{a}_z\\ \vec{v}_2=&c_4 \hat{a}_x+c_5 \hat{a}_y+c_6 \hat{a}_z\\ \vec{v}_3=&c_7 \hat{b}_x+c_8 \hat{b}_y+c_9\hat{b}_z \end{align}\]

Write out, on paper, the derivation of each expression asked for below. Do not just show your answers or skip steps, show your process of expanding and simplifying each expression.

  1. $\vec{v}_1 \times \vec{v}_2 $
  2. \(|\vec{v}_2|\)
  3. $\vec{v}_1 \cdot \vec{v}_3 $

  4. Now assume you are given more information about the relationship between the \(A\) and \(B\) reference frames. Simplify your expression for problem 3 using \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{3}\) and

    \[\sideset{^{a}}{^{b}}R (\theta) = \begin{array}{c|ccc}& b_x& b_y& b_z\\ \hline a_x &\cos{\theta} &0& \sin{\theta} \\ a_y &0&1&0\\ a_z & -\sin{\theta} & 0& \cos{\theta} \end{array} \]

Part 2

Say you have two vectors in 3D space, \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\).

  1. How many degrees of freedom does the system of vectors have?
  2. Write an equation that constrains \(\vec{a} \perp \vec{b}\)
  3. Write an equation that constrains \(\vec{a} \parallel \vec{b}\)

  4. Now consider a third vector \(\vec{c}\), as well. Write an equation that constrains \(\vec{c}\) to be \(l\) units along the vector from \(\vec{a}\) to \(\vec{b}\).

  5. Write an equation that constrains \(\vec{c}\) to be \(l\) units away from the vector from \(\vec{a}\) to \(\vec{b}\).

Part 3

Given the following robot:

  1. Write the two incremental rotation tables, $\sideset{^{a}}{^{b}}R (\theta_1) $ and $\sideset{^{b}}{^{c}}R (\theta_2) $.
  2. Find the vector \(\vec{r}\), describing the end effector's position in terms of each link's "native" unit vectors.
  3. Resolve \(\vec{r}\) into base frame components (\(\hat{a}_x\), \(\hat{a}_y\), and \(\hat{a}_z\)).
  4. Assemble the Jacobian, \(J\), for the end effector by extracting the partial derivative of \(\vec{r}\) expressed in \(\hat{a}_x\), \(\hat{a}_y\), and \(\hat{a}_z\) in terms of \(\partial{\theta_1}=\dot{\theta}_1\) and \(\partial{\theta_2}=\dot{\theta}_2\), such that \(\begin{bmatrix}\partial\vec{r}\cdot \hat{a}_x \\ \partial\vec{r}\cdot \hat{a}_y \\\partial\vec{r}\cdot \hat{a}_z\end{bmatrix} = J\begin{bmatrix}\dot{\theta}_1\\\dot{\theta}_2\end{bmatrix}\)
  5. Now say the robot's joints move at a rate of \(\dot{{\theta}} = \begin{bmatrix}\dot{\theta}_1\\\dot{\theta}_2\end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix}2.2\\3.1\end{bmatrix}\). What is the velocity of the end-effector? (Assume \(\theta_1=\frac{\pi}{6}\),\(\theta_2=\frac{\pi}{6},l_b=1,l_c=1\))
  6. Now say a force of \(\vec{f}=500\hat{a}_x+1.7\hat{a}_y+3.2\hat{a}_z\) N is exerted on the end-effector, with \(\theta_1=\frac{\pi}{2}\),\(\theta_2=0,l_b=1,l_c=1\). What torques are required at each joint to hold the robot in static equilibrium?
  7. Please explain why, given a force of 500N in the x direction, the torques at the joints are so low.

Part 4

Create a spherical mechanism. Draw four vectors emanating from a common origin. Pick your own vectors / angles.

Assumptions:

  • Assume \(\vec{v}_1 \parallel \hat{a}_x\)
  • Assume triangle A (in blue) is fixed to the \(\hat{a}_x\),\(\hat{a}_y\) plane.
  • None of the vectors should be collinear with each other
  • The inner angles between vectors should be nonzero, in the range of \(\frac{\pi}{6}<\theta_i<\pi\)
  • Assume an initially planar mechanism: \(\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3+\theta_4=2\pi\)

Steps:

  1. Create your four-bar spherical mechanism in paper. Fold up the mechansim and observe which joints are "mountain" and which are "valley" joints as you move it through its range of motion
  2. Take a picture of your mechanism in its flattened and one folded state.
  3. Cut along \(\vec{v}_1\) to separate triangles A and D. This creates a serial, spherical linkage from A to D.
  4. Copy body A, cut it out and tape it to D along \(\vec{v}_1\).

  5. Write down the four unit quaternions(\(r_1\)-\(r_4\)) that describe the half-angle rotations about \(\vec{v}_1\), \(\vec{v}_2\), \(\vec{v}_3\), and \(\vec{v}_4\). Use the variables \(\alpha_1\)-\(\alpha_4\) to describe the rotation angles about \(\vec{v}_1\)-\(\vec{v}_4\), respectively.

    Don't forget to ensure that your quaternion meets the constraints required for unit quaternions.

  6. Write down the four pure quaternions(\(p_1\)-\(p_4\)) that describe the vectors \(\vec{v}_1\)-\(\vec{v}_4\) to points \(P_1\)-\(P_4\).

  7. For the following steps, please express each point in the A frame using quaternion operations. You don't need to expand \(r_1\)-\(r_4\) or substitute values in for \(\alpha_1\)-\(\alpha_4\) at this point.
    1. Consider \(p_3\) to be a function of a rotation about \(\vec{v}_2\) from A. Write the expression for \(p_3'\) expressed in the A frame.
    2. Consider \(p_4\) to be a function of 2 successive rotations about \(\vec{v}_2\) and \(\vec{v}_3\) from A. Write the expression for \(p_4'\) expressed in the A frame.
    3. Consider \(p_1\) to be a function of 3 successive rotations about \(\vec{v}_2\), \(\vec{v}_3\), and \(\vec{v}_4\) from A. Write the expression for \(p_1'\) expressed in the A frame.
    4. Consider \(p_2\) to be a function of 4 successive rotations about \(\vec{v}_2\), \(\vec{v}_3\), \(\vec{v}_4\), and \(\vec{v}_1\) from A. Write the expression for \(p_2'\) expressed in the A frame.
  8. Using your modified spherical mechanism, define two constraint equations that will "glue" point \(P_1'\) to the original point \(P_1\) using vector operations. Use your paper model to get a sense of the mechanism's degrees of freedom.
  9. Now, using your modified spherical mechanism, define one additional constraint equation that will "glue" \(P_2'\) to the original \(P_2\) using vector operations. Again, use your paper model to get a sense of the mechanism's degrees of freedom.
  10. In Python, using the code you began in class, define the same quaternions and quaternion operations from steps 6 and 7a-7d. Use symbolic variables from the sympy package to represent \(\alpha_1\)-\(\alpha_4\).
  11. Use your three constraint equations and \(\alpha_1 = \frac{\pi}{6}\) to solve for a valid configuration for your device (\(\alpha_1\)-\(\alpha_4\)) with scipy.optimize.minimize(). Use a physically consistent initial guess based on the "mountain" and "valley" joints you noticed earlier. Show the results of the minimization function and the resulting values for \(\alpha_1\)-\(\alpha_4\)
  12. Plot your device in 3D using matplotlib using the results of your optimization.
